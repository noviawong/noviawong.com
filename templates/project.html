Summary: RHCA stands for River House Condominium Association. To get messages across to residents, the Association currently relies on paper notice that they put up on the glass windows at each building. This method requires the owners and residents to be physically present at the building to receive new information, which is not always possible to do so (e.g., traveling). The current non-centralized communication method presents a problem: a lack of available information for all house owners and tenants, particularly, it lacks an effective, clean, and accessible information system for all. To address the need of portable searching and communication, I created a prototype for a mobile application that facilitates efficient and comprehensive searches for neighborhood information for all resident and owners at RHCA. The information users can find in this application includes the following: neighborhood files (e.g., board minutes, map, policies), neighborhood events, announcements, safety alerts, chats and messages from other users, and money transaction.

Summary: MealSpace is a solution that engages the student community that is new to a university campus by providing a physical space for students to cook together to encourage healthy eating. This space provides ingredients for tailored recipes, along with step-by-step guidance for preparing the meal. Drawing inspiration from maker spaces across the nation, MealSpace is conceptualized from studying student habits, responses, and working with the University of Michigan Health Services. Research was done with students regarding the transition to a college environment from high school or a different country, since the drastic change of setting can push students to not prioritize healthy eating. The main focus of MealSpace resides in engaging students to create a meal with each other.

Summary: Today, passwords are a commonly used strategy for protecting online accounts. These accounts may include sensitive personal information such as financial statements in a bank account, or may include nearly no identifying information such as that in a gaming application on a phone. Yet, to increase the security of such accounts, users are often required to create passwords that comply with strict criteria (e.g., a minimum of one upper case, one lower case, one special character, one number, and a minimum character length). Using a self-reported questionnaire, Shay et al. (2010) found that when users were required to create passwords under such strict requirements, they often reused old passwords with little or no modifications. Users also reported to be more likely to forget passwords created under such restrictions because of the uncommon combinations used for "high security" passwords. With a memory recall test and a self-reported questionnaire, this study investigated the effect of three different password composition requirements on users' recall of the passwords they generated, their general attitudes, self-predicted memorability, and perceived security for the requirements. Results indicated that actual memorability was not affected by the choice of password composition requirement, but participants perceived each requirement differently. This study also explored other areas of interest, such as password composition strategies.

Summary: Cyber security is often thought to be a topic in the computer science field. However, it involves more than just the codes and the framework; a secured network also requires users to be aware of the security situation and to take action. A common example is when the system alerts the users of firewall expiration through a popup notification. However, it is often difficult to know whether these alerts are influential in users’ decision-making regarding cyber security. One way to explore the extent they are influenced is to observe their behaviors when these alerts are presented to them. By using tools such as virtual machines, researchers can perform a study without harming the host system (the computer’s physical hard drive) while documenting users’ behaviors. These tools will also provide a virtual environment that stimulates a real computer setup. Virtual machines can also help researchers to understand more about how different factors (e.g., cost, time, cognitive capacity) affect users’ decisions in making cyber security choices. While this presentation focuses on how virtual machine technology can support research on human decision-making in cyber security, future studies can also utilize this technology to further improve the functionality of technology by incorporating human physical needs and their psychological needs.
