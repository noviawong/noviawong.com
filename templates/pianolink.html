PianoLink:
A Practice Tool for Adult Piano Learners

Role:
UX Researcher and Designer

Skills Used:
Observation/Field Research
Interviews
Cultural Probes
Competitive Analysis
User Enactment
Arduino/Physical Prototyping
Public Speaking/Presentation

Duration:
4 months (2018)

Summary:
Goal
Based on our observation and initial interviews with music school students and instructors, we found that college students (non-music major) and adult learners are more motivated to learn music than younger music students. As a result, we wanted to use technology to further improve their experiences in learning music, particularly piano. Our goal was to move past beyond a mobile application to something more physical and tangible, and use pervasive design methodologies to identify our users’ pain points and what they really needed.

Process
Prior to setting our goal, my team and I went through long hours of discussion of what domain to focus on. To finally settle on one specific area, we assessed our own abilities, expertise in the domain in question, along with existing solutions. Then, we utilized methods such as cultural probe to understand the users: adult piano learners We found that the main problems students face when they first learn piano are (1) unable to identify their own mistakes, and (2) lack of communication between instructor and students. We then proceeded to rapid prototyping and user enactment, where users helped us to validate and critique our designs. Finally, we prototyped our project using Arduino to further simulate the experience, and recorded a video to show the full experience of what our users could potentially have. Using both our physical prototype and video, we were able to validate our solution with piano instructors and students.

Solution
Our solution, PianoLink, is an interactive system that supplements piano instructors’ absence during a practice session. It is aimed to assist adult piano beginners to identify their own mistakes while playing, and track their progress in a measurable way. Also, it is equipped with an amazing rainbow pattern to encourage piano learners to keep practicing, along with a mobile application that allows them to share their progress with other people, including their instructors and friends. This strip also comes with lights that project directly onto the piano keys as well as a touchscreen control panel for additional controls and settings. With this solution, users will be able to identify their own mistakes with the help of 3 different modes: learning, practice, and free play mode, helping users to learn to play with gradually increasing difficulties.

Process Overview

Problem Scoping

Choosing a problem space to work with is not an easy task. My team and I went through 4 hours of brainstorming and discussions, along with 2 iterations before settling on “music” as a problem domain. The image below shows a glimpse of our brainstorming session.

To better understand this domain and some of the problems current music students may have, we interviewed 5 music school students. We identified the following key issues: (1) practice room availability, (2) lack of immediate feedback from professors, and (3) lack of motivation for practicing.

We soon became interested in exploring the early relationship between piano instructors and students at the early stages of learning. Since piano students range from children to adults, we had to narrow down our target user group. We had easier access to sampling adult students, so we started with this group of users. As a result, we are interested in learning about what frustrations they have even though they are highly motivated to learn.

We found that when students practice piano without the instructors’ presence, they often play the music over and over again to figure out finger placement and note accuracy on their own. This process involves an extremely high level of self-reflection and self-teaching skills without direct and immediate feedback from their instructors. That said, our question became:

How can we help adult beginner piano students to practice efficiently on their own?

While we began our user research phase, we also took the time to brainstorm possible solutions. We first sketched individually among the 4 of us, then came back together to discuss the concepts that we came up with. After reviewing all the sketches, we settled on the following:

01 | Projection on keyboard

02 | Haptic sound wave feedback for hands position

03 | Real-time visualization play accuracy feedback

User Research

We needed to understand our users more before settling on one specific solution. To do so, the following methodologies were used: interviews, cultural probes, and competitive analysis. Interviews and cultural probes will allow us to understand our users’ current relationships with practicing piano, while competitive analysis would provide us with insight on other types of tools that are used by piano players right now.

Our research design was driven by the following research questions:

  • What are some of the key emotions students have with piano learning?
  • How do students currently practice piano and self-reflect when instructor isn’t present?
  • Particularly, what types of tools do they use to aid them?
  • What challenges do students and instructors face when students start learning piano?
  • What is the role of a piano instructor in a student’s learning process?
  • What are the types of communication and collaboration piano students currently have with their instructors?

Interview

Our main goals for the interview were to understand the participants more, learn about how long they had been playing and taking lesson, and why they wanted to start learning in the first place. These interviews were a follow-up on those who finished a cultural probe with us. We conducted 2 interviews with 2 different first year piano students. Both of them are graduate students at U-M, and are currently taking lessons through the School of Music, having one-on-one lessons with student instructors. Each interview lasted for approximately 30 to 45 minutes, and was conducted either in person or through Google Meet. We also went over the cultural probes with the two interviewees and understood more about their reasoning behind each activity results.

01 | Progress tracking
Through interviews, we found that piano beginners often set themselves a goal for practice sessions. They wanted to play the music notes accurately and play by the proper tempo. However, both beginners and instructors recognized that beginner piano students often have trouble identifying their own mistakes or tracking their own progress. As an instructor put it, students have trouble “diagnosing their own symptoms, which then failing to provide themselves a prognosis”.


Quote: “Sometimes I read the notes wrong and I never find out until she [the instructor] points it out."

Quote: “I don't like using the metronome. My instructor uses clapping to help me. It's hard to play and count at the same time."

Cultural Probe

As a group, we decided to conduct a cultural probe for both piano instructors and students. We recruited 5 participants, with 2 of them being the piano instructors and 3 beginner piano students. Given the locations of piano instructors -- some were completing the study remotely -- I suggested that we use a digital cultural probe instead of a physical cultural probe. As a result, we put together a digital cultural probe kit using Google Slides.

We were able to conduct the following activities:

  • Picture collage
  • Map your emotions
  • Create 10 hashtags
  • Rate the difficulty of various activities
  • Circle your current communications

02 | Communication
We found that piano students have positive opinions regarding piano instructors. They viewed their instructors as very positive, patient, and encouraging. Yet, we also found that communication typically doesn’t happen outside of the in-person piano lessons, which means students and instructors don’t have a chance to talk to each other regarding the practice progress until lesson the next week.

03 | Physical Resources
Although piano instructors constantly try to incorporate technology into their teaching, physical resources are still extremely important Learning piano often require physical resources such as books and worksheets. Through our cultural probe, we found that when students think of resources they use when they learn and practice piano, they often think of their physical sheet music and a metronome.

04 | Emotion Roller-Coaster
Piano students found learning piano to be rewarding, but upon looking closely at their practice sessions, we found that it’s pretty much an emotion roller-coaster for them. They start out excited to practice a new piece of music, then run into frustration when they make a mistake or don’t seem to be making much of a progress, and finish the practice session off with excitement and joy if they meet their goals.

Competitive Analysis

An informal competitive analysis was conducted, in which we assessed other technological products participants mentioned that help with their learning. A few products that piano instructors and students currently use are: (1) Synthesia, (2) Simply Piano, and (3) YouTube tutorials. Other than products that students and instructors are currently using, we also identified some pervasive design we noticed before: (4)The ONE Smart Piano. These digital tools all act as tutorials for piano learners and all are either IPad applications or browser products. The ONE Smart Piano is a physical electric piano with keys that light up to guide learners in playing the right notes.>

05 | Current Technology Is Limited
Upon looking at the technology some of our interviewees and participants mentioned, we found that they are fairly limited to iPad or phone applications. Another competitor that we found requires the purchase of a physical electronic piano, which many of our target users can’t afford or commit into purchasing.

Following user research, we had our second iteration of the solutions. We took an approach similar to the first round of sketching earlier.

Digital and Physical Prototyping

Now that we had more understanding of our users and the problems they were facing. We wanted to select one concept from earlier stages and test it with our users. Prior to designing our physical prototype for our first round of testing, my team and I set up a list of criteria to follow. Examples include:

  • Acceptability: Will this be accepted in an area that seemingly does not use much technology?
  • Pervasiveness: Does it integrate well into the environment that they currently practice in?

We refined our concepts from earlier stage and picked a final one.

User Enactment

The validation process was guided by the following research questions:

  • What are some of the key emotions people have while using our prototype?
  • Will this tool efficiently help aid people practicing piano at different stages of learning a musical piece?
  • Does this tool help to combat the main challenges that students face when they first start learning piano?

To develop the protocol for our user enactment, we followed the Speed-dating Matrix protocol and wrote scenarios that fit along the 3 design dimensions we deemed important: (1) Players’ relationship with the piece (New vs. In Progress), (2) Goal of the practice session, and (3) Reactivity of the system.

Some of the cells were left empty on purpose, because the scenarios were highly unlikely to occur.
We tested the study with 6 different users. Five of our participants had some experiences with playing piano, while 1 had absolutely no experiences with playing piano or any other musical instrument. This participant was able to provide us with perspective that we had never thought of, as a true piano beginner. Here is a breakdown of our participants:

In most of our UE sessions, our setup included a cardboard piano, paper screens for a mobile app, Q-tips as replacement for LED lights, and a cardboard LED strip. In situations when we could not secure a physical piano, we used a cardboard piano with matching piano key dimensions to simulate the experiences.

After each scenario, the user went through a debriefing for that particular scenario regarding their experiences with the prototype and scenario. Debriefing questions included questions such as “how did you feel about that particular experience” and “if you could change something in that scenario, what would you change”, along with other follow-up questions regarding the observations we made during the session.

We did a small affinity wall after our user enactment and found the following insight:

01 | Expertise level differences
A true beginner who doesn’t know how to read the notes on a sheet music would need significantly more guidance than someone who has been practicing piano for a while, or those who play another music instrument. Their differences in expertise level greatly influenced how they reacted to the LED light guidance.

02 | Analysis and comparison are helpful
All our participants found that being able to compare their own performance across different practice sessions -- or even within one practice session -- to be helpful in their piano learning journey. One of them even expressed the interest in seeing more types of analysis in addition to note accuracy, tempo consistency, and overall performance. Examples of additional analyses include dynamics, pedal usage, and finger positions.

03 | Automation is frowned upon
None of our participants appreciated the full automation when we had our experience prototyping. They hated and found the idea of automatic sharing distasteful, and deemed it as an act of privacy violation. Many of them showed confusion when the system automatically adjusted the difficulty level for them (e.g., tempo). All of them preferred to have more control over what they could do with the LED strip as well as the accompanying mobile application.

04 | LED lights alone can be distracting
When we prototyped the LED lights with Q-tips, our participants found them to be distracting and they focused on the lights instead of reading the sheet music. They reasoned that this is because the lights were “installed” directly above the keys, and way below the sheet music, so they weren’t sure if they were supposed to follow the lights or the notes on the sheet music.

We wanted to explore how we could subsidize for the absence of an instructor during practice session as much as possible. During the next phases of ideation and design, whenever we were stuck, we would ask ourselves a question:

What would a piano instructor do?

Final Solution

01 | Portable add-on product with smart guidance
PianoLink features a foldable strip which can be easily attached to an existing piano and put into a backpack. It’s equipped with LED mini projectors and a touchscreen control panel and can be connected to the user’s smart phone, which allows piano students to have a more effective and engaging practice session. This will be especially useful for students who have to go to shared practice rooms or those who cannot afford a smart piano with augmented assistance.

02 | Different modes for piano students with different levels of expertise
Based on the dynamic nature of piano learning, PianoLink brings flexibility in terms of guidance by having 3 modes for users: (1) learning mode, (2) practice mode, and (3) free-play mode.
Learning mode will guide the students through the song one note at a time, through LED lights being projected onto the piano keys. The wrong note will light up in red color and the correct note will light up in green. Also, before they start playing the notes, the users will be told what fingers to be placed on which keys by looking at 5 different lights lighting up.
The practice mode will show the students which is the right note to play only when they play a wrong note. There will also be clapping sounds appearing only when the users aren’t following the tempo they set, to let them get a sense of how fast they’re supposed to play the music with.
In the free-play mode, the student will be able to continue to track progress without PiankLink’s guidance.

03 | Track progress and provide analytics insights on teaching with the instructor
PianoLink enables the student user to record the practice and track the learning process, with the user’s consent. As the physical product is synced with the mobile app, the user will be able to see how they’re making progress through the mobile app. They could decide to share the information, which would help the instructor to understand the student’s strengths and weaknesses and adjust the curriculum accordingly.

04 | Delightful learning experience with Positive Reinforcement
Students can set a goal for the practice session on their mobile app (ex. Finish the song without making any mistake). The LED strip will display in a sequence of rainbow colors to cheer them up. The positive reinforcement can help with motivating adult students who’re practicing on their own and possibly struggling with some hurdles before mastering the instrument.

Feedback and Reflection

Limitation of the design
Some of the limitation of our current design has to deal with the level of control from the instructor’s side. As of right now, instructors still don’t have control over students’ goals at their practice sessions. Communication between instructors and students will still be minimal even with PianoLink.
The design is also too futuristic, and some parts cannot be realized immediately with current technology. (e.g., recognizing the notes, comparing the performance, data collected from practices)

Insights after prototyping
We started to ask ourselves the question" How might we provide feedback to address users' emotional needs?" After prototyping and our demo, we realized that we aren't doing enough to address this part of the research findings. We’ve incorporated rainbow LED lighting up to indicate achievements. But, this is just a smaller reminder that they are doing well and letting the beautiful display be something to works towards as well as a symbol of achievement. We considered an audio feedback to encourage the users. This would indicate feedback and encouragement such as “good job,” “keep it up,” or “almost there!” Throughout research, we were able to find that learning is a pretty personal and emotional process. It can be frustrating yet very rewarding. Our goal is to make sure that the user keeps making progress feels that rewarding feeling more often. As a result, this will be a future step we want to focus on.

Feedback
The design received positive feedback from piano instructors. One piano instructor was excited to see where this project may head to in the future, and is willing to contribute in any way she could.
