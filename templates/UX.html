Duration:4 months, 2018

Summary:
Goal
Our goal for this project was to create a patient-friendly bariatric decision aid that would both educate and assist patients to understand their surgical options and their potential outcomes and risks of each option. A local health informatics company gave us this project on redesigning their web-based bariatric surgery outcomes calculator. At first, my team and I had the goal of simply redesigning the web-page to make it more user-friendly for the patients. But then, after our research, we decided on a new goal instead.

Process
Multiple research methodologies were either attempted or used to collect user experiences and preferences. First, we conducted stakeholder interviews and received a demonstration of the outcomes calculator. Then, we attempted to schedule interviews with physicians and bariatric patients. Our attempt in trying to recruit our primary users failed, largely because of the sensitivity of the topic. As a result, we took a different approach and focused on completing our competitive analysis and turned to literature reviews on patients’ decision making process, as well as existing user experience from former bariatric patients. I led the UX research effort in finding blog posts and news articles about bariatric patients’ experiences in making a decision regarding which surgical option to choose from. From there onward, I created a persona that fit the model, and the persona had guided the design and our presentation ever since. Once the research stage was completed, I led the design phase and mentored a junior UX designer to create wireframes and high fidelity click-through prototypes with Figma. My team and I validated with 8 different people using our high fidelity prototype, and we presented to our colleagues at University of Michigan, our client, and even the VP of Technology at Snapchat.

Solution
We took statistical components from the original outcomes calculator and embedded them into a patient-facing online decision aid. Patients would be able to use this decision aid independently, while learning about potential surgical options. Several differences between the original calculator and our decision aid include: (1) decision aid spans across different web pages, easing patients into the process instead of the one-page calculator, (2) decision aid takes into account patients’ lifestyle choices and gives a more holistic view of each surgical option than the original calculator, (3) decision aid offers a general route or a personalized route, taking patient privacy and resistance into account, (4) decision aid presents surgery risks and outcomes in a more visual form compared to the statistical and number-driven calculator, and (5) there are more visual information and media to aid patients in understanding their options, risks, and outcomes.

Summary:
Goal
Design a solution to improve college students' eating habits, including motivating them to eat nutritiously and plan their meals better.

Process
My team and I focused on grocery shopping habits at first. After conducting contextual inquiry, diary studies, surveys, and talking to professionals, we soon found that grocery shopping was not the problem college students face in unhealthy eating. As a result, we looked at prior research with college students regarding their transition to a college environment from high school or a different country. We found that since the drastic change of setting often require a lot of students' time and mental capacity, this change can push students to not prioritize healthy eating over for example, making new friends. We further studied our users by talking to sophomores and juniors at universities, and by consulting with nutritionist at the University Health Service.

Solution
Drawing inspiration from maker spaces across the nation, MealSpace is a solution that engages the student community that is new to a university campus by providing a physical space for students to cook together to encourage healthy eating. This space provides ingredients for tailored recipes, along with step-by-step guidance for preparing the meal. The technical intervention focuses on a website for registration of cooking sessions and a tablet interface for recipes. The main focus of MealSpace resides in engaging students to create a meal with each other.


Role: Primary Investigator and Researcher

Skills Used:

Duration: 5 months (2017)

Summary:
Goal
Investigate the effect of password composition requirements on users’ recall ability, attitudes, self-predicted memorability, and perceived security for such requirements.

Process
I first conducted literature review to assess the critical issues in the domain and explore past studies that looked at similar topics. Moving onwards, I worked with my advisor to narrow down the problem statement and hypotheses for this thesis. After I created my hypotheses for this project, I began to reference past studies and created my own experiment. My advisor and I agreed that an implicit memory test would be the most beneficial for my goal. As a result, I created an implicit memory test that was embedded in the survey I sent to my participants at San Jose State University. After 2 to 3 weeks of data collection, I used Excel and SPSS to analyze the survey data and looked at statistical differences that either supported or rejected my hypotheses. My final thesis went through several versions of iteration before passing through the thesis review committee.

Article Abstract
Today, passwords are a commonly used strategy for protecting online accounts. These accounts may include sensitive personal information such as financial statements in a bank account, or may include nearly no identifying information such as that in a gaming application on a phone. Yet, to increase the security of such accounts, users are often required to create passwords that comply with strict criteria (e.g., a minimum of one upper case, one lower case, one special character, one number, and a minimum character length). Using a self-reported questionnaire, Shay et al. (2010) found that when users were required to create passwords under such strict requirements, they often reused old passwords with little or no modifications. Users also reported to be more likely to forget passwords created under such restrictions because of the uncommon combinations used for "high security" passwords. With a memory recall test and a self-reported questionnaire, this study investigated the effect of three different password composition requirements on users' recall of the passwords they generated, their general attitudes, self-predicted memorability, and perceived security for the requirements. Results indicated that actual memorability was not affected by the choice of password composition requirement, but participants perceived each requirement differently. This study also explored other areas of interest, such as password composition strategies.

Duration:4 months (2018)

Summary:
Goal
Based on our observation and initial interviews with music school students and instructors, we found that college students (non-music major) and adult learners are more motivated to learn music than younger music students. As a result, we wanted to use technology to further improve their experiences in learning music, particularly piano. Our goal was to move past beyond a mobile application to something more physical and tangible, and use pervasive design methodologies to identify our users’ pain points and what they really needed.

Process
Prior to setting our goal, my team and I went through long hours of discussion of what domain to focus on. To finally settle on one specific area, we assessed our own abilities, expertise in the domain in question, along with existing solutions. Then, we utilized methods such as cultural probe to understand the users: adult piano learners We found that the main problems students face when they first learn piano are (1) unable to identify their own mistakes, and (2) lack of communication between instructor and students. We then proceeded to rapid prototyping and user enactment, where users helped us to validate and critique our designs. Finally, we prototyped our project using Arduino to further simulate the experience, and recorded a video to show the full experience of what our users could potentially have. Using both our physical prototype and video, we were able to validate our solution with piano instructors and students.

Solution
Our solution, PianoLink, is an interactive system that supplements piano instructors’ absence during a practice session. It is aimed to assist adult piano beginners to identify their own mistakes while playing, and track their progress in a measurable way. Also, it is equipped with an amazing rainbow pattern to encourage piano learners to keep practicing, along with a mobile application that allows them to share their progress with other people, including their instructors and friends. This strip also comes with lights that project directly onto the piano keys as well as a touchscreen control panel for additional controls and settings. With this solution, users will be able to identify their own mistakes with the help of 3 different modes: learning, practice, and free play mode, helping users to learn to play with gradually increasing difficulties.

Duration: 4 months (2017)

Summary:
RHCA stands for River House Condominium Association. To get messages across to residents, the Association currently relies on paper notice that they put up on the glass windows at each building. This method requires the owners and residents to be physically present at the building to receive new information, which is not always possible to do so (e.g., traveling). The current non-centralized communication method presents a problem: a lack of available information for all house owners and tenants, particularly, it lacks an effective, clean, and accessible information system for all. To address the need of portable searching and communication, I created a prototype for a mobile application that facilitates efficient and comprehensive searches for neighborhood information for all resident and owners at RHCA. The information users can find in this application includes the following: neighborhood files (e.g., board minutes, map, policies), neighborhood events, announcements, safety alerts, chats and messages from other users, and money transaction.
